plainEnglish: >-
  An IRS rule that disallows a tax loss on a security if you buy the same (or a
  "substantially identical") security within 30 days before or after the sale.
  The loss isn't gone — it's rolled into the cost basis of the replacement.
detailedExplanation: >-
  Codified in Section 1091 of the Internal Revenue Code, the wash sale rule
  prevents investors from claiming a tax loss while effectively staying in the
  same position. If you sell a security at a loss and, within a 61-day window
  (30 days before the sale, the day of the sale, and 30 days after), you or
  your spouse (or an account you control) buys the same or a substantially
  identical security, the IRS disallows the loss. The disallowed amount is
  added to the cost basis of the replacement shares and the holding period
  carries over — so you recover the tax benefit when you ultimately sell the
  replacement without triggering another wash sale. The "you or your spouse"
  and "any account you control" pieces are the ones that trip people up —
  crucially, buying the identical security in a Traditional or [Roth IRA](/term/roth-ira)
  triggers the rule and permanently disallows the loss (because you can't
  recover basis inside an IRA).
workedExample: >-
  You sell 100 shares of Fund X at a $5,000 loss on November 1. On November
  20, you buy 100 shares of Fund X in your IRA. The $5,000 loss is disallowed
  — and because the replacement is in an IRA, it's permanently lost, not
  merely deferred. Contrast that with buying Fund X back in the same taxable
  account: the $5,000 is added to your cost basis in the new shares, so you
  recover the benefit whenever you eventually sell those shares at a profit
  (or a smaller loss).
usedInSentence: "\"I swapped into a different total-market fund to realize the loss without triggering the wash sale rule.\""
whenItApplies: >-
  Any time you sell a stock, bond, [ETF](/term/exchange-traded-fund), or mutual fund at a loss in a taxable
  account and want to claim the loss. Also relevant if your spouse — or any
  account you control, including IRAs — buys the same security near the sale
  date.
commonMistakes: >-
  Replacing the sold security in an IRA or [401(k)](/term/401k) within 30 days (permanently
  disallowing the loss); forgetting that dividend reinvestment counts as a
  purchase and can trigger the rule; assuming two similar [index funds](/term/index-fund) are
  automatically safe to swap (the IRS has never fully defined "substantially
  identical," so funds tracking the same exact index invite scrutiny —
  practitioners typically swap between funds tracking different but similar
  indexes).
howToRemember: >-
  "Thirty days on each side." The sale is in the middle of a 61-day window;
  buying the same thing anywhere inside that window washes out the loss.
whenToConsult: >-
  A CPA before any significant tax-loss harvesting sweep, especially if you
  have automatic dividend reinvestment on, multiple brokerage accounts, or
  household members who invest in similar funds. The rule is deceptively easy
  to trip on and hard to unwind.
faqs:
  - question: Does the wash sale rule apply to crypto?
    answer: "As written, no — Section 1091 refers to \"stock or securities,\" which crypto arguably isn't. [verify — Congress has periodically proposed extending the rule to crypto; check current law before relying on this.]"
  - question: What does "substantially identical" mean?
    answer: The IRS has never given a precise test. Identical CUSIPs clearly qualify; two ETFs tracking the same index are widely considered risky; two ETFs tracking different but similar indexes are the standard harvesting swap.
  - question: If the loss is disallowed, is it gone forever?
    answer: Usually no — the disallowed amount is added to the replacement's cost basis, so you recover the benefit on the next sale. The exception is when the replacement sits in an IRA or 401(k), where basis tracking is meaningless and the loss is permanently lost.
  - question: Does the rule apply to gains?
    answer: No. It only disallows losses. Realizing a gain and buying the security right back has no special tax consequence (beyond owing the tax).
