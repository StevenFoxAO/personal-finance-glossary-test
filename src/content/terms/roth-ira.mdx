lastUpdated: 2026-04-22
plainEnglish: >-
  An individual retirement account you fund with after-tax dollars. You pay no tax on
  the growth or on qualified withdrawals in retirement.
detailedExplanation: >-
  A Roth IRA is a retirement account in which contributions are made with money
  you've already paid income tax on, so the government doesn't tax it again on the
  way out — neither the contributions nor their investment gains — provided you
  follow the rules (generally, age 59½ and a five-year holding period for earnings).
  Direct contributions are subject to annual dollar limits and to income-based
  phase-outs that can reduce or eliminate your ability to contribute. Contributions
  (but not earnings) can be withdrawn at any time, tax- and penalty-free, which makes
  a Roth more flexible than most retirement accounts.
workedExample: >-
  Say you contribute $7,000 [verify current limit] per year from age 30 to 65 and
  average a 7% annual return. Your account grows to roughly $968,000 — and because
  it's a Roth, the entire balance is yours tax-free in retirement.
usedInSentence: "\"I maxed out my Roth IRA for the year before contributing anything to my taxable brokerage account.\""
whenItApplies: >-
  Anyone with earned income within the IRS income limits can contribute. Roths are
  especially attractive if you expect to be in the same or higher tax bracket in
  retirement than you are today.
commonMistakes: >-
  Contributing while over the income limit without realizing it (creates an excess
  contribution that must be corrected); withdrawing earnings before 59½ or before
  the five-year clock is up and owing tax plus a penalty; assuming the Roth always
  beats a Traditional IRA — it doesn't if your current bracket is much higher than
  your retirement bracket will be.
howToRemember: >-
  "Pay now, never pay again." Roth = Taxes Right Out of the gate, then free.
whenToConsult: >-
  Talk to a CPA or CFP® if your income is near the phase-out range, if you're
  thinking about a Roth conversion, or if you have both Traditional and Roth
  balances and want to model the long-term tax impact.
faqs:
  - question: What's the current Roth IRA contribution limit?
    answer: "[verify current limit] The IRS updates this most years. Check irs.gov/retirement-plans for the current year."
  - question: Can I contribute if I also have a 401(k)?
    answer: Yes. IRA and 401(k) limits are separate, though very high earners may be phased out of direct Roth IRA contributions.
  - question: Do I have to take RMDs from a Roth IRA?
    answer: No. Original Roth IRA owners are not required to take RMDs during their lifetime, unlike Traditional IRAs.
  - question: Can I withdraw my contributions early?
    answer: Yes — your own contributions (not earnings) can be withdrawn at any time, tax- and penalty-free.
