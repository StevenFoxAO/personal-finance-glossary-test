name: Backdoor Roth IRA

lastUpdated: 2026-04-22
plainEnglish: >-
  A legal workaround that lets high earners get money into a Roth IRA when their
  income is too high to contribute directly. You make a nondeductible Traditional
  IRA contribution, then convert it to a Roth.
detailedExplanation: >-
  [Roth IRA](/term/roth-ira) contributions phase out above certain income levels. The "Backdoor
  Roth" sidesteps this by using two steps that are each individually allowed: a
  nondeductible contribution to a [Traditional IRA](/term/traditional-ira) (no income limit), followed by
  a Roth conversion (also no income limit). The IRS has publicly acknowledged
  this path. The catch is the pro-rata rule: if you already hold pre-tax dollars
  in any Traditional, SEP, or SIMPLE IRA, the conversion is treated as partly
  taxable in proportion to those balances — which can make the Backdoor Roth far
  less attractive.
workedExample: >-
  You contribute $7,000 [verify current limit] of after-tax money to a Traditional
  IRA and immediately convert it to a Roth. With no other pre-tax IRA balances,
  the conversion is tax-free. If you had $63,000 of pre-tax IRA money on hand,
  only 10% of the conversion ($700) would be tax-free; the other $6,300 would be
  taxed as ordinary income.
usedInSentence: "\"Because I'm over the Roth income limit, I do a Backdoor Roth every January.\""
whenItApplies: >-
  Earners whose income exceeds the Roth IRA direct-contribution limit and who
  have little or no existing pre-tax IRA balances.
commonMistakes: >-
  Ignoring the pro-rata rule; forgetting to file IRS Form 8606 to report the
  nondeductible contribution and the conversion; letting significant earnings
  accrue in the Traditional IRA before converting (those earnings are taxable).
howToRemember: >-
  Front door closed? Same building, back door. The IRS sees you either way and
  the IRA rules still apply.
whenToConsult: >-
  A CPA is essentially required the first time you do this — both to confirm the
  pro-rata math and to file Form 8606 correctly. A mistake here can create
  double-taxation that's hard to unwind.
faqs:
  - question: Is the Backdoor Roth legal?
    answer: Yes — the IRS has publicly acknowledged the strategy, though Congress could change the rules.
  - question: What if I already have a big Traditional IRA?
    answer: Pre-tax balances trigger the pro-rata rule. Some people roll those balances into a 401(k) first to clear the path.
  - question: Do I have to report it?
    answer: Yes — Form 8606 reports the nondeductible contribution and the conversion.
relatedTerms:
