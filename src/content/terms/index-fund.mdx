name: Index Fund

lastUpdated: 2026-04-22
plainEnglish: >-
  A fund that doesn't try to beat the market. It just tries to match the
  performance of a specific market index — like the S&P 500 — by holding the
  same stocks in roughly the same proportions.
detailedExplanation: >-
  An index fund is a pooled investment vehicle (usually a mutual fund or [ETF](/term/exchange-traded-fund))
  built to track a published index. Instead of paying a team of analysts to pick
  stocks, the fund mechanically holds the index's constituents. That simplicity
  translates into very low operating costs (low [expense ratios](/term/expense-ratio)), low portfolio
  turnover (tax-efficient in taxable accounts), and broad diversification.
  Decades of academic research have shown that, over long time horizons, the
  majority of actively managed funds fail to beat their benchmark net of fees —
  which is the core argument for using index funds as the default building
  block of a portfolio.
workedExample: >-
  A $10,000 investment in an S&P 500 index fund with a 0.03% expense ratio costs
  you $3 per year in fund expenses. The same $10,000 in an actively managed
  large-cap fund charging 0.85% costs $85 — nearly 30× more — before any
  difference in performance.
usedInSentence: "\"I just put my 401(k) in a couple of low-cost index funds and stopped worrying about it.\""
whenItApplies: >-
  Appropriate as a core holding for virtually any long-term investor. Less
  useful for very short horizons where stock-market volatility is a concern.
commonMistakes: >-
  Picking index funds solely by name without checking the underlying index
  (two "S&P 500" wrappers can have meaningfully different expense ratios and
  tax efficiency); switching in and out based on market moves; confusing broad
  index funds with thematic or leveraged "index" products.
howToRemember: >-
  "Own the haystack." Instead of looking for the needle (a winning stock), you
  own the whole haystack cheaply.
whenToConsult: >-
  A fiduciary advisor if you're trying to coordinate index funds across taxable
  and tax-advantaged accounts (asset location) or if you inherited a portfolio
  of individual positions and need a careful unwind plan.
faqs:
  - question: Are index funds the same as ETFs?
    answer: Not exactly. Many ETFs are index funds, but ETFs are a wrapper and can hold either index-tracking or actively managed strategies.
  - question: What's a typical index-fund expense ratio?
    answer: "Total-market and S&P 500 index funds are often available at 0.03%–0.10% [verify]."
  - question: Can an index fund lose money?
    answer: Yes. It tracks the index, which can decline — especially in the short run.
