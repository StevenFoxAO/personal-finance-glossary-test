plainEnglish: >-
  An employer-sponsored retirement plan that lets you save a portion of each
  paycheck — often matched by your employer — with significant tax advantages.
detailedExplanation: >-
  A 401(k) is a workplace retirement plan named after the section of the tax code
  that created it. You choose a percentage of each paycheck to contribute, and
  many employers match some portion of what you contribute. Traditional 401(k)
  contributions come out pre-tax (lowering this year's taxable income) and are
  taxed on withdrawal; Roth 401(k) contributions are after-tax and come out
  tax-free in retirement if rules are met. Investment choices are limited to the
  menu your plan offers. Contribution limits are set annually by the IRS and are
  much higher than IRA limits. Early withdrawals before 59½ typically owe income
  tax plus a 10% penalty.
workedExample: >-
  If you earn $100,000 and contribute 10% ($10,000) to a Traditional 401(k), and
  your employer matches 50% up to 6% of pay, you'd add $10,000 plus a $3,000
  match — $13,000 total going in for the year.
usedInSentence: "\"Always contribute at least enough to your 401(k) to get the full employer match — it's free money.\""
whenItApplies: >-
  Anyone working for an employer that sponsors a 401(k). Self-employed people have
  a close cousin called a Solo 401(k).
commonMistakes: >-
  Leaving employer match money on the table; staying in expensive default funds
  when cheaper index funds are offered; cashing out the balance when changing
  jobs instead of rolling it over.
howToRemember: >-
  401(k) is a tax-code section number, not a product name. Think of it as the
  workplace wrapper around your retirement investments.
whenToConsult: >-
  A CFP® can help you decide between Traditional and Roth contributions based on
  your expected future tax bracket, and evaluate rollover options when you change
  jobs.
faqs:
  - question: What's the current contribution limit?
    answer: "[verify current limit] The IRS publishes annual 401(k) elective-deferral limits; catch-up contributions apply at age 50 and above."
  - question: Should I contribute to a 401(k) or an IRA first?
    answer: Typically, contribute to the 401(k) up to the full employer match first, then consider an IRA, then return to the 401(k) beyond the match.
  - question: Can I borrow from my 401(k)?
    answer: Many plans allow loans, but borrowing reduces the time your money spends compounding and creates risk if you leave the job before repayment.
