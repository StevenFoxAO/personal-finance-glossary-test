plainEnglish: >-
  How you divide your investment portfolio among different types of assets —
  mostly stocks, bonds, and cash. It's the single biggest decision about your
  portfolio's risk and return.
detailedExplanation: >-
  Stocks, bonds, and cash behave very differently: stocks offer higher long-run
  expected returns with higher short-term volatility; bonds dampen volatility
  and produce income; cash preserves principal but loses purchasing power to
  inflation over time. Classic research (Brinson et al., 1986, and subsequent
  follow-ups) has long argued that asset allocation — not individual security
  selection — explains the majority of the variation in portfolio returns.
  The "right" allocation depends on your time horizon, your ability and
  willingness to tolerate drawdowns, and your goals. Within broad asset
  classes, allocation also extends to sub-categories: domestic vs. international
  stocks, government vs. corporate bonds, short vs. long duration, and so on.
workedExample: >-
  A 40-year-old with a 25-year horizon might choose an 80/20 stock-to-bond mix.
  A 65-year-old retiree drawing from the portfolio might prefer 50/50 or 60/40
  to reduce the risk of a bad market at the wrong time.
usedInSentence: "\"We rebalanced back to our target allocation — stocks had drifted up to 82% and we wanted to be closer to 70%.\""
whenItApplies: >-
  Every long-term investor. The decision matters most when the portfolio is
  large relative to future contributions — in other words, the older you get
  and the more you've saved, the more asset allocation drives your outcomes.
commonMistakes: >-
  Choosing an allocation based on recent returns (chasing what just did well);
  picking an allocation you can't stick with during a bear market and selling
  at the bottom; forgetting to rebalance; ignoring allocation across accounts
  rather than looking at the household portfolio as a whole.
howToRemember: >-
  "The mix matters more than the pick." Getting the stock/bond split roughly
  right matters more than which index fund you chose.
whenToConsult: >-
  A fiduciary financial planner when approaching retirement, during major life
  changes, or when your portfolio has grown large enough that "close enough"
  targets no longer feel adequate.
faqs:
  - question: What's a typical rule of thumb?
    answer: "\"110 minus your age\" as a rough stock percentage is a common starting point, but it's just a rough heuristic — not advice."
  - question: How often should I rebalance?
    answer: Either on a schedule (once or twice a year) or when allocations drift past a threshold (e.g., 5 percentage points).
  - question: Does asset allocation include cash?
    answer: Yes, though most investors keep cash (emergency fund, near-term needs) separate from their long-term portfolio allocation.
