name: Step-Up in Basis
slug: step-up-in-basis
categories:

lastUpdated: 2026-04-22
plainEnglish: >-
  When someone dies, the cost basis of the assets they owned generally resets to
  the market value on the date of death. If heirs sell immediately, there's
  little or no capital gains tax on decades of appreciation.
detailedExplanation: >-
  Cost basis is what you paid for an asset. Capital gains tax is owed on the
  difference between what you paid and what you sell for. When an asset passes
  to an heir at death, its basis is "stepped up" to the fair market value on the
  date of death — wiping out the embedded gain for capital gains tax purposes.
  This is a significant feature of the U.S. tax code and a reason many
  professionals recommend holding highly appreciated assets until death rather
  than gifting them during life (gifted assets carry the giver's basis forward).
  Community-property states can allow a "double step-up" on jointly held spousal
  assets. Assets in IRAs and 401(k)s do not receive a step-up.
workedExample: >-
  A parent bought stock decades ago for $20,000. At death it's worth $200,000.
  The heir's basis becomes $200,000. If the heir sells the next day at $205,000,
  the capital gain is only $5,000 — the $180,000 of prior appreciation escapes
  capital gains tax.
usedInSentence: "\"Don't gift the appreciated stock — if you hold it until death, the kids get a step-up in basis.\""
whenItApplies: >-
  Inherited taxable-account assets (stocks, real estate, private business
  interests). Retirement-account assets are treated differently.
commonMistakes: >-
  Gifting highly appreciated assets during life and losing the step-up;
  forgetting to document date-of-death values (especially for real estate and
  private businesses); not realizing community-property rules can double the
  step-up for surviving spouses.
howToRemember: >-
  "Death resets the clock." For capital gains purposes, the heir starts fresh at
  the date-of-death value.
whenToConsult: >-
  A CPA and estate-planning attorney before any significant lifetime gift of
  appreciated property, and immediately after a death to establish basis with
  proper appraisals.
faqs:
  - question: Do retirement accounts get a step-up?
    answer: No. Traditional IRAs and 401(k)s keep their pre-tax character; heirs pay ordinary income tax on distributions.
  - question: What's a "double step-up"?
    answer: In community-property states, both halves of a jointly held spousal asset can step up when the first spouse dies.
  - question: Does the step-up work for gifts?
    answer: No. Gifted assets transfer with the giver's original basis (carryover basis), not a step-up.
