plainEnglish: >-
  Everything you own minus everything you owe. One number that summarizes your
  financial position at a point in time.
detailedExplanation: >-
  Net worth is the sum of your assets (cash, investments, retirement accounts,
  home, cars, business interests) minus the sum of your liabilities (mortgage,
  student loans, credit cards, auto loans, etc.). It's a snapshot, not a
  performance measure — what really matters is the trend over time. Growing
  net worth means your financial position is improving; a flat or falling net
  worth is a signal to examine savings rate, debt management, and investment
  strategy. The number alone isn't meaningful without context: a low net worth
  for a 25-year-old can be healthy while a similar number for a 60-year-old
  may warrant attention.
workedExample: >-
  Assets: home ($450,000), [401(k)](/term/401k) ($120,000), brokerage ($40,000), cash
  ($15,000), car ($18,000) = $643,000. Liabilities: mortgage ($310,000), car
  loan ($9,000), student loan ($22,000), credit cards ($3,000) = $344,000.
  Net worth = $643,000 − $344,000 = $299,000.
usedInSentence: "\"I track net worth quarterly — it's the one number that tells me whether everything else is working.\""
whenItApplies: >-
  Everyone. Most people should calculate it at least once a year, with many
  preferring quarterly.
commonMistakes: >-
  Including possessions at purchase price rather than realistic resale value
  (especially cars); ignoring illiquid assets like a private business, which
  inflates net worth until you try to sell; confusing cash flow with net worth.
howToRemember: >-
  "Own minus owe." That's it. Everything else is commentary.
whenToConsult: >-
  A CFP® when net worth crosses thresholds that trigger new planning needs —
  estate-tax exposure, concentrated stock positions, small-business succession,
  or liability-protection concerns.
faqs:
  - question: Should I include my primary home?
    answer: Yes, but be honest about its value — market comps, not what you hope it's worth.
  - question: How often should I update it?
    answer: Quarterly is a reasonable default; monthly is fine too. Less often than annually loses the useful signal.
  - question: What's a "good" net worth?
    answer: It depends on your age, income, goals, and where you live. Trend matters more than absolute number.
