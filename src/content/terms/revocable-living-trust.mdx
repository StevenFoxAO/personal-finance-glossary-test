name: Revocable Living Trust
slug: revocable-living-trust
categories:
  - estate-planning
lastUpdated: 2026-04-22
plainEnglish: >-
  A legal container you create during your lifetime to hold your assets, that you
  can change or cancel anytime. Its main job is to pass those assets to your
  heirs without going through probate court.
detailedExplanation: >-
  You (the grantor) create the trust, name yourself as trustee, and transfer
  assets into the trust — houses, accounts, business interests. You keep full
  control: you can amend, restate, or revoke the trust anytime during your
  lifetime. You also name a successor trustee who takes over at your death or
  incapacity. Because the trust, not you personally, "owns" the assets, they pass
  according to the trust document rather than through probate, which is a public,
  court-supervised process that can be slow and expensive in some states. A
  revocable trust does not provide tax benefits while you're alive — for income
  tax, it's essentially ignored, and its assets are still in your taxable estate.
workedExample: >-
  A couple in California with a $1.5M home and brokerage accounts creates a
  revocable living trust, retitles the home and accounts in the trust's name,
  and names their adult child as successor trustee. When they die, the child
  distributes the assets per the trust document — no probate case, no public
  filings of the estate's contents.
usedInSentence: "\"We set up a revocable living trust so our kids won't have to deal with probate in California.\""
whenItApplies: >-
  Most useful for owners of real estate (especially in states with expensive or
  slow probate, like California and Florida), people with privacy concerns, or
  anyone wanting smoother management if they become incapacitated.
commonMistakes: >-
  Creating the trust and then failing to "fund" it — i.e., not actually
  retitling assets into its name; assuming it avoids estate tax (it doesn't);
  forgetting to update it after major life events like marriage, divorce, or
  moving states.
howToRemember: >-
  "Revocable = reversible." You can unwind or change it any day of the week. The
  trust is a bucket with your name on it; probate is the alternative line you're
  skipping.
whenToConsult: >-
  An estate-planning attorney licensed in your state. DIY trust kits can produce
  documents that look fine but fail at funding, successor-trustee powers, or
  state-law quirks. Pair with a CPA if you have complex assets.
faqs:
  - question: Does a revocable trust avoid estate tax?
    answer: No. Trust assets remain part of your taxable estate for federal and state estate-tax purposes.
  - question: Do I still need a will?
    answer: Yes — typically a "pour-over" will, which sweeps anything missed into the trust at death.
  - question: Is a revocable trust the same as an irrevocable trust?
    answer: No. An irrevocable trust cannot (easily) be changed and has different tax and asset-protection properties.
relatedTerms:
