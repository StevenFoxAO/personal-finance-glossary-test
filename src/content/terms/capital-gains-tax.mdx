plainEnglish: >-
  The tax you owe on the profit when you sell an investment (or other capital
  asset) for more than you paid.
detailedExplanation: >-
  A capital gain is the difference between what you sell an asset for and its
  cost basis (what you paid, adjusted for things like reinvested dividends). If
  you've held the asset for more than one year, it's a long-term capital gain and
  gets preferential federal tax rates — currently 0%, 15%, or 20% depending on
  taxable income [verify current brackets]. Holdings for one year or less produce
  short-term gains, which are taxed at ordinary income rates. High earners may
  also owe the Net Investment Income Tax of 3.8% on top. Losses offset gains of
  the same type first; unused net losses can offset up to $3,000 of ordinary
  income per year, with the rest carried forward.
workedExample: >-
  You bought [ETF](/term/exchange-traded-fund) shares for $10,000 and sold them 18 months later for $14,000 — a
  $4,000 long-term gain. In the 15% bracket, you'd owe $600 in federal capital
  gains tax (state tax may also apply).
usedInSentence: "\"I'm waiting another month to sell — I don't want to trigger short-term capital gains tax.\""
whenItApplies: >-
  Whenever you sell an appreciated asset in a taxable (non-retirement) account.
  Gains inside [401(k)s](/term/401k) and IRAs aren't taxed annually.
commonMistakes: >-
  Selling just before the one-year mark and paying ordinary-income rates when a
  few more days would have qualified the sale for long-term rates; forgetting
  that reinvested dividends raise your cost basis; overlooking state-level
  capital gains tax.
howToRemember: >-
  "Long and lazy wins." Longer holding = lower rate.
whenToConsult: >-
  A CPA before selling anything with large embedded gains — the tax-planning
  options (installment sales, charitable gifts, tax-loss harvesting) can be worth
  far more than the fee.
