name: Expense Ratio
slug: expense-ratio
categories:
  - investing-markets
lastUpdated: 2026-04-22
plainEnglish: >-
  The annual percentage of a fund's assets that gets taken out to pay the fund's
  operating costs. You never see it billed — it's just deducted from the fund's
  return.
detailedExplanation: >-
  An expense ratio of 0.50% means that for every $10,000 invested in the fund,
  about $50 a year is quietly removed to cover management, administration,
  custodian fees, and marketing. Because the fee comes out of returns, it
  compounds negatively over time: a 1% difference in expense ratio, sustained
  over 30 years, can easily cost you a quarter of your ending balance. That's
  why low-cost index funds and ETFs have reshaped the industry. Expense ratios
  do not include brokerage commissions or, for mutual funds, any sales loads or
  transaction fees.
workedExample: >-
  Two investors each put $100,000 into S&P 500 funds and earn 7% before fees
  for 30 years. Investor A's fund charges 0.05%; Investor B's charges 1.00%. A
  ends with roughly $751,000; B ends with roughly $574,000. The 0.95%
  difference in expense ratio costs B about $177,000.
usedInSentence: "\"I won't buy any fund with an expense ratio over 0.20% unless there's a really good reason.\""
whenItApplies: >-
  Anytime you buy a mutual fund or ETF. It's often the single most predictive
  number about a fund's long-run net performance.
commonMistakes: >-
  Ignoring the expense ratio because "fund performance is what matters" — past
  performance doesn't repeat, but fees are forever; focusing on expense ratio
  alone while ignoring trading costs, bid-ask spreads, or hidden load fees.
howToRemember: >-
  "A small leak sinks a great ship." Tiny percentages compound into large
  dollars over decades.
whenToConsult: >-
  A fee-only advisor can help benchmark whether a fund's expense ratio is in
  line with its peers and whether any higher cost is actually buying you
  something useful.
faqs:
  - question: What's considered "low"?
    answer: "Under 0.20% is typically considered low; broad index funds and ETFs are often 0.03–0.10% [verify]."
  - question: Is the expense ratio deducted from my account?
    answer: It's taken out of the fund's assets — reflected in the fund's share price and returns — rather than billed to you directly.
  - question: Do ETFs charge expense ratios?
    answer: Yes. Both mutual funds and ETFs report expense ratios.
