name: Dollar-Cost Averaging
slug: dollar-cost-averaging
categories:

lastUpdated: 2026-04-22
plainEnglish: >-
  Investing a fixed dollar amount on a regular schedule, regardless of what the
  market is doing. You buy more shares when prices are low and fewer when
  prices are high.
detailedExplanation: >-
  Dollar-cost averaging (DCA) is the default way most people already invest —
  every paycheck contribution to a 401(k) is DCA. Its main appeal is
  behavioral: by committing to a schedule, you avoid the temptation to time the
  market and the anxiety of deploying a large sum all at once. Mathematically,
  if you have a lump sum to invest and the market's long-run expected return is
  positive, lump-sum investing historically beats DCA about two-thirds of the
  time because you're in the market longer. The case for DCA over a lump sum is
  behavioral (reducing regret risk), not one of expected return.
workedExample: >-
  You invest $500 monthly. The fund price is $50 in month 1 (10 shares), $40 in
  month 2 (12.5 shares), $60 in month 3 (8.33 shares). You've invested $1,500
  and now own 30.83 shares at an average cost of $48.66 per share — lower than
  the $50 average of the three prices, because your fixed dollars bought more
  shares when the price was low.
usedInSentence: "\"I'd rather dollar-cost average the bonus over six months than invest it all on one scary day.\""
whenItApplies: >-
  The default for ongoing contributions from earned income. Often used as a
  behavioral tool when deploying a windfall, even though lump-sum has a
  slight historical edge on expected return.
commonMistakes: >-
  Stopping DCA during market drops (when the approach is working best);
  confusing DCA with a market-timing strategy; stretching a lump-sum deployment
  so long that the cash drag undermines the purpose.
howToRemember: >-
  "Same dollars, different days." The schedule is the strategy.
whenToConsult: >-
  A CFP® is worth consulting when deploying a large windfall — they can help
  model the expected-value vs. regret-risk tradeoff between lump sum and DCA.
faqs:
  - question: Does DCA beat lump-sum investing?
    answer: Historically, no — not on expected return. But it can be easier to stick with psychologically.
  - question: How often should I contribute?
    answer: Whatever cadence matches your income. Monthly or per-paycheck is typical.
  - question: Should I stop contributing when the market drops?
    answer: Usually no. Declining prices mean your fixed contribution buys more shares — the core mechanic of DCA.
