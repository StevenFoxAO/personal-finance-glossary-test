plainEnglish: >-
  The person (or people) you name directly on an account to receive its balance
  when you die. These designations override whatever your will says.
detailedExplanation: >-
  Retirement accounts (IRAs, [401(k)s](/term/401k)), life insurance policies, annuities, and
  many bank and brokerage accounts let you name beneficiaries directly on the
  account. When you die, the account passes to those beneficiaries by contract,
  bypassing probate and overriding your will. Accounts typically allow primary
  beneficiaries and contingent (backup) beneficiaries. Failing to name a
  beneficiary — or naming an outdated one — is one of the most common and most
  expensive estate-planning mistakes. Rules also differ by account type:
  ERISA-covered 401(k)s generally require a spouse to be the primary beneficiary
  unless the spouse signs a waiver.
workedExample: >-
  You named your spouse the primary beneficiary on your 401(k) twenty years ago
  and never updated it. You divorced, remarried, and had children, but never
  changed the form. At your death, the 401(k) goes to the ex-spouse — regardless
  of what your will says.
usedInSentence: "\"Update your beneficiary designations after any major life event: marriage, divorce, birth, death.\""
whenItApplies: >-
  Any account that offers a beneficiary form — especially retirement accounts and
  life insurance.
commonMistakes: >-
  Leaving the beneficiary blank (assets then default to the account's rules, often
  the estate — which means probate); naming a minor child directly instead of
  through a trust or custodian; not updating after divorce; not coordinating
  designations with the overall estate plan.
howToRemember: >-
  "Beneficiary beats the will." The account's form is what the custodian reads
  first.
whenToConsult: >-
  An estate-planning attorney if you want to direct assets through a trust for
  minor children, beneficiaries with special needs, or second-marriage
  situations. Even without a lawyer, audit designations every few years and
  after major life events.
faqs:
  - question: Does my will control my 401(k)?
    answer: No. The beneficiary designation on the 401(k) controls, regardless of what your will says.
  - question: Can I name a trust as beneficiary?
    answer: Yes, but the rules around trusts as retirement-account beneficiaries are technical — get professional help.
  - question: What if I don't name anyone?
    answer: The account's default rules apply. Often the asset ends up in the estate and passes through probate.
