lastUpdated: 2026-04-22
plainEnglish: >-
  When the interest (or investment return) you earn gets added to your balance,
  and then starts earning its own interest. Your money begins making money on
  the money your money already made.
detailedExplanation: >-
  Compound interest (or, more broadly, compound return) is the mathematical
  engine behind long-term wealth building. Instead of earning a flat amount on
  your original principal each period, returns are reinvested so the balance
  grows exponentially. The longer the time horizon, the more pronounced the
  effect: the first decade feels slow, but the last decade can dwarf
  everything that came before. This is also why even small differences in fees
  or tax drag matter so much over long periods — the differences themselves
  compound. In debt, the same math works against you: unpaid credit-card
  balances compound in the issuer's favor.
workedExample: >-
  $10,000 invested at a 7% compound annual return becomes roughly $19,672 after
  10 years, $38,697 after 20, and $76,123 after 30. By year 30, the gain is
  $66,000 — more than six times the original deposit — with no additional
  contributions.
usedInSentence: "\"Compound interest is what turns a boring savings habit into real money — but only if you leave it alone.\""
whenItApplies: >-
  Any account or debt where returns (or interest charges) accumulate on the
  growing balance — so, almost all long-term investing and most consumer debt.
commonMistakes: >-
  Interrupting compounding by frequent withdrawals; underestimating fees and
  taxes that silently compound away from you; believing that a late start can
  be overcome by a higher return rather than more time.
howToRemember: >-
  "The snowball." A growing ball picks up more snow with each roll. Time is
  the hill; compounding is the roll.
whenToConsult: >-
  A financial planner for long-horizon projections that take taxes, inflation,
  sequence-of-returns risk, and real-world contribution patterns into account —
  simple "compound growth" calculators miss all of those.
faqs:
  - question: What's the Rule of 72?
    answer: A shortcut — divide 72 by your annual rate to estimate how many years it takes to double. At 7%, money doubles every ~10 years.
  - question: Does compounding apply to stocks?
    answer: Yes. Reinvested dividends and price appreciation compound over time.
  - question: Why is starting early so valuable?
    answer: Early dollars get the most time to compound. A dollar invested at 25 has 10 more doublings than a dollar invested at 35.
