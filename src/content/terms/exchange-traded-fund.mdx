name: Exchange-Traded Fund (ETF)
slug: exchange-traded-fund
categories:
  - investing-markets
lastUpdated: 2026-04-22
plainEnglish: >-
  A pooled investment fund that trades on a stock exchange like a single stock.
  You buy and sell shares of the fund during market hours at the market price.
detailedExplanation: >-
  An ETF holds a basket of underlying assets — often stocks or bonds — and issues
  shares that trade on exchanges throughout the day, unlike mutual funds which
  price once daily. Most ETFs are passively managed to track an index, though
  actively managed ETFs exist. A structural feature called "in-kind creation and
  redemption" allows large market makers to exchange baskets of securities for
  ETF shares (and vice versa), which tends to keep the share price close to the
  fund's net asset value and makes ETFs more tax-efficient than comparable
  mutual funds. ETFs typically have low expense ratios and no minimum beyond one
  share.
workedExample: >-
  You buy 10 shares of a total-market ETF at $250 each, for $2,500. If the
  fund's 0.03% expense ratio is in line with typical broad ETFs, you'd pay
  about $0.75 per year in fund expenses on that $2,500 position.
usedInSentence: "\"I use ETFs in my taxable account because they're more tax-efficient than most mutual funds.\""
whenItApplies: >-
  Useful for investors who want intraday liquidity, tax efficiency in taxable
  accounts, or access to specialty strategies not available as mutual funds.
commonMistakes: >-
  Buying leveraged or inverse ETFs without understanding they're designed for
  short-term trading and decay over time; using market orders on thinly traded
  ETFs and getting a bad price; assuming all ETFs are low-cost index funds
  (some actively managed and thematic ETFs carry much higher fees).
howToRemember: >-
  "Mutual fund wearing sneakers." Same basic idea, but it runs during the
  trading day.
whenToConsult: >-
  A tax professional before large ETF trades in a taxable account, especially
  if you're using ETFs for tax-loss harvesting (pairing similar-but-not-identical
  funds to avoid wash sales).
faqs:
  - question: Are ETFs better than mutual funds?
    answer: Not universally. ETFs tend to be more tax-efficient in taxable accounts; mutual funds can be fine inside 401(k)s and IRAs.
  - question: Can I buy fractional ETF shares?
    answer: Many brokers now allow fractional ETF shares; check your platform.
  - question: Do ETFs pay dividends?
    answer: Yes, when the underlying holdings pay dividends — typically quarterly.
