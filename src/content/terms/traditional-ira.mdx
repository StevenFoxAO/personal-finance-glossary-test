lastUpdated: 2026-04-22
plainEnglish: >-
  A retirement account you fund with pre-tax dollars (usually). You get a potential
  tax deduction now, the money grows tax-deferred, and you pay ordinary income tax
  on every dollar you withdraw.
detailedExplanation: >-
  A Traditional IRA lets eligible savers contribute up to an annual limit and, in
  many cases, deduct that contribution from taxable income for the year. The
  account's investments grow without being taxed each year. Withdrawals in
  retirement are taxed as ordinary income. Whether the up-front deduction is
  available depends on whether you (or a spouse) are covered by a workplace
  retirement plan and on your income. Withdrawals before age 59½ usually trigger
  income tax plus a 10% early-withdrawal penalty, with narrow exceptions.
  Traditional IRAs are subject to Required Minimum Distributions starting at age 73
  [verify current age under SECURE 2.0].
workedExample: >-
  You contribute $7,000 [verify current limit] and are in the 24% federal bracket.
  The deduction cuts your current-year tax bill by about $1,680. Later, if you
  withdraw $10,000 in retirement while in the 22% bracket, you'd owe about $2,200
  of federal income tax on that withdrawal.
usedInSentence: "\"I'm using my Traditional IRA for the deduction now and my Roth for flexibility later.\""
whenItApplies: >-
  Most useful when your current tax bracket is higher than you expect it to be in
  retirement, or when you want to reduce taxable income this year.
commonMistakes: >-
  Assuming the deduction is always available (it phases out for higher earners
  with workplace plans); forgetting about RMDs; confusing a nondeductible
  contribution with a Roth contribution.
howToRemember: >-
  "Tax deferred, not tax free." You get a break now, pay the bill later.
whenToConsult: >-
  Talk to a CPA before mixing deductible and nondeductible contributions in the
  same IRA — the pro-rata rule gets confusing fast, especially if a Backdoor Roth
  is on the table.
faqs:
  - question: Is the contribution always deductible?
    answer: No. Deductibility phases out at higher incomes if you or your spouse are covered by a workplace retirement plan.
  - question: When must I start taking withdrawals?
    answer: Required Minimum Distributions currently begin at age 73 [verify under SECURE 2.0 Act].
  - question: Can I have both a Traditional and a Roth IRA?
    answer: Yes, but the combined contributions across both cannot exceed the single annual IRA limit.
